SCRIPT FIDELITY — this matters most:
- Use ONLY the dialogue lines given in the scene. Copy each spoken line VERBATIM into that shot's `dialogue` — never paraphrase, shorten, rewrite, translate, or invent a line.
- Cover the scene's dialogue IN ORDER. Assign each provided line to exactly one shot. A shot may fold two short consecutive lines from the same quick exchange, but never drop a line, never reorder them, and never merge lines from different speakers into one.

CHARACTERS:

- `subject`: the ONE character the shot is primarily about — whose face and reaction the audience is meant to watch. Every shot has a subject.
- `foreground_characters`: the subset of `characters_in_frame` who are present ONLY as a foreground occlusion — a back or shoulder to camera in an over-the-shoulder framing, or a silhouette — so their FACE is not seen. BLOCKING — keep the geography consistent so shots cut together and feel like one space:

Put important positions in `notes`, e.g. "child crouched at the low table, mother in the foreground on the phone".
- When two characters share the scene, prefer angles that show their real spatial relationship (over-the-shoulder, two-shot) rather than isolating each on the same flat background.

BLOCKING — geometry the camera can execute. For EVERY character in frame, fill `subjects` with ABSOLUTE positions: depth (`frame_position`), which third of the frame (`screen_side`), `facing` and `eyeline`. Resolve relational prose BEFORE writing — "Sol backs away from the figure" must become absolute terms for BOTH parties (e.g. Sol foreground screen-right facing screen-left stepping backward toward camera; the figure background screen-left facing screen-right advancing). DIALOGUE COVERAGE — for a shot that speaks a `dialogue` line: prefer coverage where the speaker's mouth is NOT sharply readable — over-the-shoulder with the speaker's back to camera, three-quarter or profile angles, the LISTENER's reaction while the line plays, or a motivated cutaway. A clean front-facing close framing (MCU/CU) of a speaker is allowed ONLY when that speaker is alone in frame — those shots can be mouth-driven downstream. Never stage a front-facing speaking mouth inside a two-shot.

REACT-THEN-REVEAL — an entrance, discovery or reversal beat (a door bursts open, a figure appears, someone sees something shocking) is TWO consecutive shots, never one crammed frame:
- Shot A: the REACTING character alone, face readable, eyeline OFF-camera toward where the threat or arrival will be — on the screen side the stage map has established for it (or establishes now). Do NOT show what they see.
- Shot B: immediately after, WHAT they see — the reveal — staged on the very side Shot A's eyeline pointed to, the revealed person as `subject`, the reactor at most a foreground shoulder.
Give both shots `subjects` whose screen sides and eyelines agree, so the cut reads as one continuous look. Budget the pair as two shots.

EVENT DECOMPOSITION — a beat that contains a STATE CHANGE (a collapse, faint, injury, attack, death, transformation, crash, explosion) can never live inside one shot: a single 5-second clip cannot show before, during and after, so the video model picks one state at random and fills the rest with its safe defaults. Board the event as 2-3 CONSECUTIVE single-state shots instead:
- (optional) the BASELINE state: the world as it was the instant before (the performer mid-song, the room at peace).
- the DURING state: the event already underway as one stable image (face contorted, hand crushing the chest, knees buckling) — the shot OPENS in this state, never transitions into it.
- the AFTERMATH state: the changed world held still (the body motionless on the floor, the crowd reacting).
Each shot's `action` describes ONE stable state in concrete physical detail — never the words "suddenly", "begins to", or "starts to" inside a single shot.

VIOLENCE BY IMPLICATION — video models sanitize graphic violence into wrong footage (a stabbing becomes a hug). For a violent or lethal beat, NEVER board the act itself; board its edges, the way real film does: the setup (the raised weapon, the advancing figure), the reaction (the victim's face, a bystander recoiling), the aftermath (a slumped figure, a motionless hand, an overturned chair). The cut between those shots IS the violence.

CONSISTENCY — every shot in this scene shares ONE world. Give ALL shots the SAME `lighting` and SAME `colour_mood` (time of day and light source do not change mid-scene). Vary `shot_type`, `camera_movement` and angle freely for visual interest, but keep the environment, lighting and colour identical so the clips cut together cleanly.
