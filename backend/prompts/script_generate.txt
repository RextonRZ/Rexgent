{development}

Structure requirements:
- Use standard screenplay format (INT./EXT. headings, character names in caps before dialogue)
- NAMES ARE FIXED: every character the premise or story brief names keeps EXACTLY that name ("Theo" stays Theo, never renamed to John). The user cast these people; renaming them breaks the drama they asked for. Invent names only for characters the premise did not name.
- A named animal the premise treats as a main character (a pet the story follows) is a real ON-SCREEN presence: write stage directions that SHOW it (in someone's arms, in its hutch, found under a bush), scene by scene — never reduce it to something only talked about.
- Match the number of scenes to the target length: roughly one short scene per 15 seconds (so a 30-second episode has about 2 scenes, a 300-second episode about 20). Use at least 1 scene. Keep a short drama tight — do not pad.
- DIALOGUE BUDGET — screen time is spoken time: one line of dialogue plays about 5 seconds on screen, and every silent action beat adds a few more. Each episode holds AT MOST {line_budget} lines of dialogue TOTAL across all of its scenes, each line under 15 words. When the story wants more, cut or merge lines instead of exceeding the budget — a short drama says less and shows more. An episode that overshoots its budget runs long and gets cut off.
- Every character introduced must serve the plot
- Dialogue should sound natural, not expository
- When the story moves between locations, WRITE THE MOVE as a brief stage direction (they leave the cabin; the path through the trees; the door at the entrance) — the storyboard can only show a journey the script wrote, otherwise characters teleport between places.

VERTICAL MICRO-DRAMA FORMAT — this is a phone-screen short drama fighting a scroll, not a film:
- THE HOOK (first 3 seconds): Scene 1 must open MID-TENSION — a confrontation already underway, a dangerous act in progress, a shocking line, or a charged question. NEVER open with waking up, arriving somewhere, scenery, or characters introducing themselves. A viewer who sees only the first 3 seconds must need to know what happens next.
- CLIFFHANGER EVERY EPISODE: every episode except the last must END on an unresolved turn — a revelation cut short, a threat appearing, a door opening on the wrong person, a decision withheld. Write the cliffhanger as the episode's final scene, not as a summary. The final episode resolves the arc (a last sting or open question is allowed if it fits the tone).
- PACING: enter every scene as late as possible and leave it as early as possible. Each scene must raise the stakes or reveal something new; if a scene only maintains the status quo, cut it. Escalate beat by beat toward each episode's cliffhanger.

Visual feasibility (AI video) — AI video cannot keep a picture-inside-the-frame or readable writing consistent between shots, so DO NOT build the story around a prop that must show SPECIFIC, recognizable content:
- Avoid beats that hinge on a photograph or portrait of a particular person, an ID card, a wanted poster, or a phone/computer/TV screen showing a specific face, or a mirror reflecting a specific absent person. These re-generate differently every shot.
- Avoid notes, letters, texts, or signs whose specific words matter (e.g. a warning note). Convey the message through DIALOGUE or VOICEOVER (a character reads it aloud, or we hear their inner voice) — never a close-up of the writing, which comes out as garbled, inconsistent text.
- To evoke someone who isn't present, use dialogue, memory, or a brief flashback that shows them IN PERSON — not a recurring close-up of their picture.
- An unnamed presence (a figure in a doorway) may appear ONCE, e.g. as a cliffhanger, and stay nameless — and it must stay SILENT: never give it a dialogue line, not even off-screen (O.S.). A nameless label above a line of dialogue becomes a cast member downstream and breaks casting. But write its stage direction as a CONCRETE person: build, clothing and posture that fit the story's world ("a broad-shouldered figure in a rain-soaked work coat fills the doorway, backlit, face lost in shadow") — never the bare word "silhouette", which downstream renders as a random invented person. But the MOMENT a character recurs (appears in more than one scene), speaks, or matters to the plot, give them a REAL proper name — a first name or full name that fits the setting (e.g. "Jae-won", "Marco Reyes", "Detective Halloran"). NEVER use as a name: a bare common noun ("Man", "Woman", "The Girl", "The Boss"), an article label ("The Stranger"), or a RELATIONAL / POSSESSIVE descriptor ("their brother", "the mother", "her son", "the neighbour", "the old man"). A relationship is context, not a name — the brother is still "Tae-oh", the mother is still "Sun-hee". A generic label becomes that character's literal name everywhere downstream: it renders as on-screen text, it breaks name handling (stripping "Man" also deletes the ordinary word "man" from prompts), and casting cannot lock a stable face onto it. Every character with dialogue or a recurring presence gets a genuine given name, antagonists and family members included.
- If such a prop is truly unavoidable, have it appear ONCE, briefly, never in close-up, and keep it indistinct.
